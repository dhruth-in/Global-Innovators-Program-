Day 1 - Unpacking Design Thinking

Published on Sep 18, 2025 by DN

Hello Innovators! What an insightful start to our Global Innovator's Program! Today's session, "Introduction to Design Thinking" with Daniel Ling, truly opened my eyes to a structured yet incredibly flexible approach to tackling challenges – whether they're societal, business, or purely operational. If there's one core message I'm taking away, it's this: Design Thinking isn't just for designers; it's a critical skillset for anyone looking to innovate effectively. And honestly, the stats from the World Economic Forum about creative and analytical thinking being the top future skills really underscore why we're focusing on this. It's a need, not just a "want."

Why Design Thinking is Our Secret Weapon

Daniel highlighted that Design Thinking is a human-centered approach to innovation. This really resonated with me. In the past, I might have jumped straight to solutions, but this methodology forces us to slow down and truly understand the people we're designing for. It's about empathy, asking the right questions, and not being afraid to challenge the status quo – traits that definitely showed up on my "Design Thinking Checklist" self-assessment!

We looked at the "Design + Think" equation, and it's clear it's about blending actionable plans with crucial mindsets. It's not just about what we do, but how we think.

The 5 Stages: A Roadmap for Innovation

The lecture broke down the Design Thinking process into five key stages, and I can already see how each step is vital:

  • Empathize: Stepping into Their Shoes.

    This is where we really get to know our users. It's about deep understanding – observing, interviewing, and truly listening to uncover their needs, desires, and frustrations. Daniel emphasized that this is the foundation; without genuine empathy, our solutions might miss the mark. For our program, this means getting out there and connecting with the communities or stakeholders we aim to impact.

  • Define: Pinpointing the Core Challenge.

    After gathering all that rich data, this stage is about making sense of it. We consolidate our insights to craft a clear, actionable "Point of View" (POV) statement. This helps us focus our efforts. Instead of a vague problem, we define something specific like, "Our [User] needs to [User's Need] because [Insight]." This clarity is crucial before jumping to solutions.

  • Ideate: Unleashing Creativity.

    This is where the fun begins! With a well-defined problem, we're encouraged to brainstorm wildly, generate a multitude of ideas, and defer judgment. The "How Might We" questions are fantastic for sparking creativity and pushing us beyond conventional thinking. Daniel stressed building on each other's ideas and not being afraid of "bad" ideas – every idea has the potential to spark something brilliant.

  • Prototype: Making Ideas Tangible (Quickly!).

    My favorite part – making ideas real, fast and cheap! Prototypes aren't meant to be perfect; they're learning tools. Whether it's a sketch, a cardboard model, or a simple wireframe, the goal is to get something into users' hands quickly. This really connects with the "willing to fail early and often" trait I identified as a weakness – this process makes it a strength!

  • Test: Learning and Iterating.

    The final stage is about gathering feedback on our prototypes from real users. This isn't about proving our solution is perfect, but about learning how to make it better. It's an iterative loop, meaning we go back to previous stages, refine our understanding, generate new ideas, and prototype again based on what we've learned. This continuous improvement is key to true innovation.

My Role in the Journey: Strength and Growth

Looking at my own strengths (generating many new ideas and not being afraid to share) and weaknesses (willing to fail early and often), I can see how this program, grounded in Design Thinking, will be transformative. I'm excited to contribute my ideation skills and, more importantly, to embrace the "fail fast, fail often" mantra to learn and grow.

Daniel also introduced us to different problem-solving styles – Ethnographer, Kickstarter, Collaborator, Ideator, Improver, Integrator, Implementer. It was fascinating to see how each role contributes uniquely to the design thinking journey. I'm curious to see which roles emerge naturally within our teams and how we can leverage our collective strengths.

Design Thinking isn't just a process; it's a mindset that prioritizes empathy, collaboration, and continuous learning. I'm incredibly excited to apply these principles throughout our Global Innovator's Program and see the impact we can make.
